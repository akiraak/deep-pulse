: Puget Consumers Co-op

**Puget Consumers Co-op**, [doing business as](/wiki/Doing_business_as "Doing business as"){.mw-redirect} **PCC Community Markets**, is a [food cooperative](/wiki/Food_cooperative "Food cooperative") based in [Seattle, Washington](/wiki/Seattle,_Washington "Seattle, Washington"){.mw-redirect}. With over 117,000 members, it is the largest [consumer-owned food cooperative](/wiki/Consumers%27_co-operative "Consumers' co-operative") in the United States.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}3[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-3)^ Both members and non-members may shop at the retail locations, but members receive certain discounts. The organization currently operates sixteen retail locations. Nine of the sixteen stores are located in Seattle (in the [Fremont](/wiki/Fremont,_Seattle,_Washington "Fremont, Seattle, Washington"){.mw-redirect}, [Green Lake Village](/wiki/Green_Lake,_Seattle,_Washington "Green Lake, Seattle, Washington"){.mw-redirect}, [Green Lake Aurora](/wiki/Green_Lake,_Seattle,_Washington "Green Lake, Seattle, Washington"){.mw-redirect}, [Columbia City](/wiki/Columbia_City,_Seattle,_Washington "Columbia City, Seattle, Washington"){.mw-redirect}, [View Ridge](/wiki/View_Ridge,_Seattle,_Washington "View Ridge, Seattle, Washington"){.mw-redirect}, [West Seattle](/wiki/West_Seattle,_Seattle,_Washington "West Seattle, Seattle, Washington"){.mw-redirect}, [Ballard](/wiki/Ballard,_Seattle "Ballard, Seattle"), [Downtown Seattle (Corner Market)](/wiki/Downtown_Seattle,_Seattle,_Washington "Downtown Seattle, Seattle, Washington"){.mw-redirect}^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}4[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-4)^ and [Central District](/wiki/Central_District,_Seattle "Central District, Seattle") neighborhoods). The West Seattle location reopened on October 2, 2019.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}5[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-5)^ The other seven are located in [Issaquah](/wiki/Issaquah,_Washington "Issaquah, Washington"), [Kirkland](/wiki/Kirkland,_Washington "Kirkland, Washington"), [Burien](/wiki/Burien,_Washington "Burien, Washington"), [Bothell](/wiki/Bothell,_Washington "Bothell, Washington"), [Redmond](/wiki/Redmond,_Washington "Redmond, Washington"), [Edmonds](/wiki/Edmonds,_Washington "Edmonds, Washington") and [Bellevue](/wiki/Bellevue,_Washington "Bellevue, Washington").

The organization was founded in 1953; it rebranded to **PCC Natural Markets** in 1998 and then to its current name in 2017.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}6[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-6)^

## Organization {#Organization}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=PCC_Community_Markets&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Organization")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

PCC is a member-owned and operated cooperative. The members govern through established bylaws and yearly elect a board of trustees who represent the interests of the members. Like other grocery cooperatives, the profits from the retail store operations go directly back into the stores or to the community (through classes, education or charitable efforts).

Current PCC programs include:

-   PCC *Sound Consumer* newspaper
-   PCC Cooks classes
-   PCC Farmland Trust, a [501(c)(3)](/wiki/501(c)(3) "501(c)(3)"){.mw-redirect} dedicated to [sustainable](/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture "Sustainable agriculture") [organic farming](/wiki/Organic_farming "Organic farming")

Candidates on the board of trustees are required to collect 1,000 member signatures to appear on ballots. As of 2021^[\[update\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=PCC_Community_Markets&action=edit){.external .text}^, it has 90,000 members.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}7[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-Times-Bid-7)^ Two candidates in the 2021 election, both employees endorsed by the [United Food and Commercial Workers](/wiki/United_Food_and_Commercial_Workers "United Food and Commercial Workers") union, alleged that store managers had called the police on them while gathering signatures in an attempt to dissuade support.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}7[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-Times-Bid-7)^ The employees were ultimately included in the ballot and won election to the board of trustees, becoming the first store employees to sit on the board since the early 2000s.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}8[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-8)^

## Relationship with Central Co-op {#Relationship_with_Central_Co-op}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=PCC_Community_Markets&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Relationship with Central Co-op")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

In 1978, Capitol Hill Co-op dissolved for financial reasons and, in keeping with the principle that co-operatives cooperate with other cooperatives, PCC agreed to provide technical and financial assistance to the Central Co-op to replace it.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}9[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-9)^

## Pricing {#Pricing}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=PCC_Community_Markets&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Pricing")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

According to *[Consumers\' Checkbook](/wiki/Consumers%27_Checkbook "Consumers' Checkbook")* magazine, PCC\'s prices for the limited number of comparable items available were higher than the big-chain average.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}10[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-10)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}11[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-11)^ However, the quality of PCC\'s fresh produce and meat received very high scores. The prices of organic food at PCC were about the same as the average prices at other stores in the Puget Sound area.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}12[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-12)^
