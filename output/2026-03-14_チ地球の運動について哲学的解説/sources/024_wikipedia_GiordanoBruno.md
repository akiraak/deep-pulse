**Giordano Bruno** ([[[/[[dʒ]{title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'"}[ɔːr]{title="/ɔːr/: 'ar' in 'war'"}[ˈ]{title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows"}[d]{title="'d' in 'dye'"}[ɑː]{title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'"}[n]{title="'n' in 'nigh'"}[oʊ]{title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'"}]{style="border-bottom:1px dotted"}[ ]{.wrap}[[ˈ]{title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows"}[b]{title="'b' in 'buy'"}[r]{title="'r' in 'rye'"}[uː]{title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'"}[n]{title="'n' in 'nigh'"}[oʊ]{title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'"}]{style="border-bottom:1px dotted"}/](/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English")]{.IPA .nopopups .noexcerpt lang="en-fonipa"}]{.rt-commentedText .nowrap} [*jor-[DAH]{style="font-size:90%"}-noh [BROO]{style="font-size:90%"}-noh*](/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key"), [Italian:]{.IPA-label .IPA-label-small} [[\[dʒorˈdaːno[ ]{.wrap}ˈbruːno\]](/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian "Help:IPA/Italian")]{.IPA .nowrap lang="it-Latn-fonipa"}; [Latin](/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language"){.mw-redirect}: *Iordanus Brunus Nolanus*; born **Filippo Bruno**; February 1548^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}1[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-1)^ -- 17 February 1600) was an Italian [philosopher](/wiki/Philosopher "Philosopher"){.mw-redirect}, [poet](/wiki/Poet "Poet"), [alchemist](/wiki/Alchemist "Alchemist"){.mw-redirect}, [astronomer](/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer"), [cosmological](/wiki/Cosmological "Cosmological"){.mw-redirect} theorist, and [esotericist](/wiki/Western_esotericism "Western esotericism").^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}2[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGatti20021-2)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}3[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-3)^ He is known for his cosmological theories, which conceptually extended to include the then-novel [Copernican model](/wiki/Copernican_model "Copernican model"){.mw-redirect}. He practised [Hermeticism](/wiki/Hermeticism "Hermeticism") and took a mystical approach to exploring the universe. He proposed that the stars were distant suns surrounded by their own planets ([exoplanets](/wiki/Exoplanet "Exoplanet")), and he raised the possibility that these planets might foster life of their own, a cosmological position known as [cosmic pluralism](/wiki/Cosmic_pluralism "Cosmic pluralism"). He also insisted that the universe is [infinite](/wiki/Infinity "Infinity") and could have no centre.

Bruno was tried for [heresy](/wiki/Christian_heresy "Christian heresy"){.mw-redirect} by the [Roman Inquisition](/wiki/Roman_Inquisition "Roman Inquisition") on charges of denial of several core Catholic doctrines, including [eternal damnation](/wiki/Hell "Hell"), the [Trinity](/wiki/Trinity "Trinity"), the [deity of Christ](/wiki/Christology "Christology"), the [virginity of Mary](/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus) "Mary (mother of Jesus)"){.mw-redirect}, and [transubstantiation](/wiki/Transubstantiation "Transubstantiation"). Bruno\'s [pantheism](/wiki/Pantheism "Pantheism") was not taken lightly by the church,^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}4[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirx1997Collinge2012%5Bhttpsarchiveorgdetailshistoricaldictio0000coll_2ndedipage188_188%5D-4)^ nor was his teaching of [metempsychosis](/wiki/Metempsychosis "Metempsychosis") regarding the [reincarnation](/wiki/Reincarnation "Reincarnation") of the [soul](/wiki/Soul "Soul"). The [Inquisition](/wiki/Inquisition "Inquisition") found him guilty, and he was [burned at the stake](/wiki/Burned_at_the_stake "Burned at the stake"){.mw-redirect} in Rome\'s [Campo de\' Fiori](/wiki/Campo_de%27_Fiori "Campo de' Fiori") in 1600. After his death, he gained considerable fame, being particularly celebrated by 19th- and early 20th-century commentators who regarded him as a martyr for science. Some historians are of the opinion his heresy trial was not a response to his cosmological views but rather a response to his views on religion and the [afterlife](/wiki/Afterlife "Afterlife"),^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}5[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYates1964450-5)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}6[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-6)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}7[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-7)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}8[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-8)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}9[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-9)^ while others find the main reason for Bruno\'s death was indeed his cosmological views.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}10[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-10)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}11[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-11)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}12[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-12)^ Bruno\'s case is still considered a landmark in the history of [free thought](/wiki/Free_thought "Free thought"){.mw-redirect} and the emerging sciences.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}13[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-13)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}14[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-14)^

In addition to cosmology, Bruno also wrote extensively on the [art of memory](/wiki/Art_of_memory "Art of memory"), a loosely organised group of [mnemonic](/wiki/Mnemonic "Mnemonic") techniques and principles. Historian [Frances Yates](/wiki/Frances_Yates "Frances Yates") argues that Bruno was deeply influenced by the presocratic [Empedocles](/wiki/Empedocles "Empedocles"), [Neoplatonism](/wiki/Neoplatonism "Neoplatonism"), Renaissance Hermeticism, and [Book of Genesis](/wiki/Book_of_Genesis "Book of Genesis")-like legends surrounding the Hellenistic conception of [Hermes Trismegistus](/wiki/Hermes_Trismegistus "Hermes Trismegistus").^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}15[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-15)^ Other studies of Bruno have focused on his qualitative approach to mathematics and his application of the spatial concepts of geometry to language.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}16[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-16)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}17[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaiber2005-17)^

### []{#Early_years.2C_1548.E2.80.931576}Early years, 1548--1576 {#Early_years,_1548–1576}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Early years, 1548–1576")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

Born Filippo Bruno in [Nola](/wiki/Nola "Nola") (a *[comune](/wiki/Comune "Comune")* in the modern-day [province of Naples](/wiki/Province_of_Naples "Province of Naples"), in the Southern Italian region of [Campania](/wiki/Campania "Campania"), then part of the [Kingdom of Naples](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples "Kingdom of Naples")) in 1548, he was the son of Giovanni Bruno (1517 -- c. 1592), a soldier, and Fraulissa Savolino (1520--?). In his youth, he was sent to [Naples](/wiki/Naples "Naples") to be educated. He was tutored privately at the Augustinian monastery there, and attended public lectures at the [Studium Generale](/wiki/Studium_Generale "Studium Generale"){.mw-redirect}.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}18[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESinger1968-18)^ At the age of 17, he entered the [Dominican Order](/wiki/Dominican_Order "Dominican Order") at the monastery of [San Domenico Maggiore](/wiki/San_Domenico_Maggiore "San Domenico Maggiore") in Naples, taking the name Giordano, after Giordano Crispo, his metaphysics tutor. He continued his studies there, completing his [novitiate](/wiki/Novitiate "Novitiate"), and [ordained](/wiki/Ordination "Ordination") a [priest](/wiki/Priesthood_in_the_Catholic_Church "Priesthood in the Catholic Church") in 1572 at age 24. During his time in Naples, he became known for his skill with the art of memory and on one occasion travelled to Rome to demonstrate his [mnemonic](/wiki/Mnemonic "Mnemonic") system before [Pope Pius V](/wiki/Pope_Pius_V "Pope Pius V") and [Cardinal Rebiba](/wiki/Scipione_Rebiba "Scipione Rebiba"). In his later years, Bruno claimed that the Pope accepted his dedication to him of the lost work *On The Ark of Noah* at this time.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}19[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-19)^

While Bruno was distinguished for outstanding ability, his taste for [free thinking](/wiki/Freethought "Freethought") and forbidden books soon caused him difficulties. Given the controversy he caused in later life, it is surprising that he was able to remain within the monastic system for eleven years. In his testimony to Venetian inquisitors during his trial many years later, he says that proceedings were twice taken against him for having cast away images of the saints, retaining only a [crucifix](/wiki/Crucifix "Crucifix"), and for having recommended controversial texts to a novice.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}20[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAquilecchiaMontanoBertrando200711-20)^ Such behavior could perhaps be overlooked, but Bruno\'s situation became much more serious when he was reported to have defended the [Arian heresy](/wiki/Arianism "Arianism"), and when a copy of the banned writings of [Erasmus](/wiki/Erasmus "Erasmus"), annotated by him, was discovered hidden in the monastery [latrine](/wiki/Latrine "Latrine"). When he learned that an [indictment](/wiki/Indictment "Indictment") was being prepared against him in Naples, he fled, shedding his [religious habit](/wiki/Religious_habit "Religious habit"), at least for a time.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}21[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-21)^

### []{#First_years_of_wandering.2C_1576.E2.80.931583}First years of wandering, 1576--1583 {#First_years_of_wandering,_1576–1583}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: First years of wandering, 1576–1583")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

Bruno first went to the Genoese port of [Noli](/wiki/Noli "Noli"), then to [Savona](/wiki/Savona "Savona"), [Turin](/wiki/Turin "Turin") and finally to [Venice](/wiki/Venice "Venice"), where he published his lost work *On the Signs of the Times* with the permission (so he claimed at his trial) of the Dominican [Remigio Nannini Fiorentino](/wiki/Remigio_Nannini_Fiorentino "Remigio Nannini Fiorentino"){.mw-redirect}. From Venice, he went to [Padua](/wiki/Padua "Padua"), where he met fellow Dominicans who convinced him to wear his [religious habit](/wiki/Religious_habit "Religious habit") again. From Padua, he went to [Bergamo](/wiki/Bergamo "Bergamo") and then across the Alps to [Chambéry](/wiki/Chamb%C3%A9ry "Chambéry") and [Lyon](/wiki/Lyon "Lyon"). His movements after this time are obscure.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}22[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-22)^

<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb">
<a href="/wiki/File:Earlierbruno.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Earlierbruno.jpg/250px-Earlierbruno.jpg" class="mw-file-element" data-file-height="503" data-file-width="383" decoding="async" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Earlierbruno.jpg/330px-Earlierbruno.jpg 1.5x" width="190" height="250" /></a>
<figcaption>The earliest depiction of Bruno is an engraving published in 1715 in Germany, presumed based on a lost contemporary portrait.<sup><a href="#cite_note-Salvestrini-1958-23"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup></figcaption>
</figure>

In 1579, Bruno arrived in [Geneva](/wiki/Geneva "Geneva"). During his Venetian trial, he told inquisitors that while in Geneva, he told the Marchese de Vico of Naples, who was notable for helping Italian refugees in Geneva, \"I did not intend to adopt the religion of the city. I desired to stay there only that I might live at liberty and in security.\"^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}24[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESinger196812-24)^ Bruno had a pair of breeches made for himself, and the Marchese and others apparently made Bruno a gift of a sword, hat, cape and other necessities for dressing himself; in such clothing, Bruno could no longer be recognised as a priest. Things apparently went well for Bruno for a time, as he entered his name in the Rector\'s Book of the [University of Geneva](/wiki/University_of_Geneva "University of Geneva") in May 1579.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}25[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting191442-25)^ But in keeping with his personality, he could not long remain silent. In August, he published an attack on the work of [Antoine de La Faye](/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_La_Faye&action=edit&redlink=1 "Antoine de La Faye (page does not exist)"){.new}[ \[[fr](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine_de_La_Faye "fr:Antoine de La Faye"){.extiw}\]]{.noprint style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"}, a distinguished professor. Bruno and the printer, Jean Bergeon, were promptly arrested.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}26[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting191444–45-26)^ Rather than apologising, Bruno insisted on continuing to defend his publication. He was refused the right to take [sacrament](/wiki/Sacrament "Sacrament").^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}27[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting191446–47-27)^ Though this right was soon restored, he left Geneva.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}28[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting191448–49-28)^

He went to France, arriving first in [Lyon](/wiki/Lyon "Lyon"), and thereafter settling for a time (1580--1581) in [Toulouse](/wiki/Toulouse "Toulouse"), where he took his doctorate in theology and was elected by students to lecture in philosophy.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}29[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting191449–52-29)^ He also attempted at this time to return to Catholicism, but was denied absolution by the Jesuit priest he approached.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}30[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting191451-30)^ When religious strife broke out in the summer of 1581, he moved to Paris.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}31[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting191453-31)^ There he held a cycle of thirty lectures on theological topics and also began to gain fame for his prodigious memory.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}32[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting191456–57-32)^ His talents attracted the benevolent attention of the king [Henry III](/wiki/Henry_III_of_France "Henry III of France"); Bruno subsequently reported:

> \"I got me such a name that King Henry III summoned me one day to discover from me if the memory which I possessed was natural or acquired by magic art. I satisfied him that it did not come from sorcery but from organised knowledge; and, following this, I got a book on memory printed, entitled *The Shadows of Ideas*, which I dedicated to His Majesty. Forthwith he gave me an Extraordinary Lectureship with a salary.\"^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}33[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting191457–58-33)^

In Paris, Bruno enjoyed the protection of his powerful French patrons. During this period, he published several works on mnemonics, including *[De umbris idearum](/wiki/De_umbris_idearum "De umbris idearum")* (*On the Shadows of Ideas*, 1582), *[Ars memoriae](/w/index.php?title=Ars_memoriae_(Giordano_Bruno)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Ars memoriae (Giordano Bruno) (page does not exist)"){.new} *[ \[[it](https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ars_memoriae "it:Ars memoriae"){.extiw}\]]{.noprint style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"} (*The Art of Memory*, 1582), and *[Cantus Circaeus](/w/index.php?title=Cantus_Circaeus&action=edit&redlink=1 "Cantus Circaeus (page does not exist)"){.new} *[ \[[it](https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantus_Circaeus "it:Cantus Circaeus"){.extiw}\]]{.noprint style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"} (*Circe\'s Song*, 1582; described at [Circe in the arts § Reasoning beasts](/wiki/Circe_in_the_arts#Reasoning_beasts "Circe in the arts"){.mw-redirect}). All of these were based on his mnemonic models of organised knowledge and experience, as opposed to the simplistic logic-based mnemonic techniques of [Petrus Ramus](/wiki/Petrus_Ramus "Petrus Ramus"), then popular.^\[*[[citation\ needed]{title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)"}](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ Bruno also published a comedy summarising some of his philosophical positions, titled *Il Candelaio* (*The Candlemaker*, 1582). In the 16th century, dedications were, as a rule, approved beforehand, and hence were a way of placing a work under the protection of an individual. Given that Bruno dedicated various works to the likes of King Henry III, Sir [Philip Sidney](/wiki/Philip_Sidney "Philip Sidney"), [Michel de Castelnau](/wiki/Michel_de_Castelnau "Michel de Castelnau") (French Ambassador to England), and possibly [Pope Pius V](/wiki/Pope_Pius_V "Pope Pius V"), it is apparent that this wanderer had risen sharply in status and moved in powerful circles.^\[*[[citation\ needed]{title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)"}](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^

### []{#England.2C_1583.E2.80.931585}England, 1583--1585 {#England,_1583–1585}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: England, 1583–1585")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

In April 1583, Bruno went to England with letters of recommendation from [Henry III](/wiki/Henry_III_of_France "Henry III of France") as a guest of the French ambassador, [Michel de Castelnau](/wiki/Michel_de_Castelnau "Michel de Castelnau"). Bruno lived at the French embassy with the lexicographer [John Florio](/wiki/John_Florio "John Florio"). There he became acquainted with the poet [Philip Sidney](/wiki/Philip_Sidney "Philip Sidney") (to whom he dedicated two books) and other members of the Hermetic circle around [John Dee](/wiki/John_Dee_(mathematician) "John Dee (mathematician)"){.mw-redirect}, though there is no evidence that Bruno ever met Dee himself. He also lectured at [Oxford](/wiki/Oxford_University "Oxford University"){.mw-redirect}, and unsuccessfully sought a teaching position there. His views were controversial, notably with [John Underhill](/wiki/John_Underhill,_Bishop_of_Oxford "John Underhill, Bishop of Oxford"){.mw-redirect}, Rector of [Lincoln College](/wiki/Lincoln_College,_Oxford "Lincoln College, Oxford") and subsequently bishop of Oxford, and [George Abbot](/wiki/George_Abbot_(Archbishop_of_Canterbury) "George Abbot (Archbishop of Canterbury)"){.mw-redirect}, who later became [Archbishop of Canterbury](/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury "Archbishop of Canterbury"). Abbot mocked Bruno for supporting \"the opinion of [Copernicus](/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus "Nicolaus Copernicus") that the earth did go round, and the heavens did stand still; whereas in truth it was his own head which rather did run round, and his brains did not stand still\",^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}34[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-34)^ and found Bruno had both plagiarized and misrepresented [Ficino](/wiki/Marsilio_Ficino "Marsilio Ficino")\'s work, leading Bruno to return to the continent.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}35[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-35)^

Nevertheless, his stay in England was fruitful. During that time Bruno completed and published some of his most important works, the six \"Italian Dialogues\", including the [cosmological tracts](/wiki/Cosmological_tract "Cosmological tract"){.mw-redirect} *La cena de le ceneri* (*The Ash Wednesday Supper*, 1584), *De la causa, principio et uno* (*On Cause, Principle and Unity*, 1584), *De l\'infinito, universo et mondi* (*On the Infinite, Universe and Worlds*, 1584) as well as *Lo spaccio de la bestia trionfante* (*The Expulsion of the Triumphant Beast*, 1584) and *[De gli eroici furori](/w/index.php?title=De_gli_eroici_furori&action=edit&redlink=1 "De gli eroici furori (page does not exist)"){.new}[ \[[it](https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_gli_eroici_furori "it:De gli eroici furori"){.extiw}\]]{.noprint style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"}* (*On the Heroic Frenzies*, 1585). Some of these were printed surreptitiously by [John Charlewood](/wiki/John_Charlewood "John Charlewood") (the title pages claim the books were printed in Venice or Paris).^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}36[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-36)^ Some of the works that Bruno published in London, notably *The Ash Wednesday Supper*, appear to have given offence. Once again, Bruno\'s controversial views and tactless language lost him the support of his friends. [John Bossy](/wiki/John_Bossy "John Bossy") has advanced the theory that, while staying in the French Embassy in London, Bruno was also spying on Catholic conspirators, under the pseudonym \"Henry Fagot\", for [Sir Francis Walsingham](/wiki/Sir_Francis_Walsingham "Sir Francis Walsingham"){.mw-redirect}, [Queen Elizabeth](/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England "Elizabeth I of England"){.mw-redirect}\'s Secretary of State.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}37[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-37)^

Bruno is sometimes cited as being the first to propose that the universe is infinite, which he did during his time in England, but an [English](/wiki/English_people "English people") [scientist](/wiki/Scientist "Scientist"), [Thomas Digges](/wiki/Thomas_Digges "Thomas Digges"), put forth this idea in a published work in 1576, some eight years earlier than Bruno.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}38[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-38)^ An infinite universe and the possibility of alien life had also been earlier suggested by [German](/wiki/Germans "Germans") [Catholic Cardinal](/wiki/Catholic_Cardinal "Catholic Cardinal"){.mw-redirect} [Nicholas of Cusa](/wiki/Nicholas_of_Cusa "Nicholas of Cusa") in \"On Learned Ignorance\" published in 1440 and Bruno attributed his understanding of multiple worlds to this earlier scholar, whom he called \"the divine Cusanus\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}39[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-39)^

### []{#Last_years_of_wandering.2C_1585.E2.80.931592}Last years of wandering, 1585--1592 {#Last_years_of_wandering,_1585–1592}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Last years of wandering, 1585–1592")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

In October 1585, Castelnau was recalled to France, and Bruno went with him.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}40[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting1914112–113-40)^ In Paris, Bruno found a tense political situation. Moreover, his 120 theses against [Aristotelian](/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") natural science soon put him in ill favour. In 1586, following a violent quarrel over these theses, he left France for Germany.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}41[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting1914189–194-41)^

<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb">
<a href="/wiki/File:Unnamed_Figures.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Unnamed_Figures.jpg/250px-Unnamed_Figures.jpg" class="mw-file-element" data-file-height="1464" data-file-width="1476" decoding="async" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Unnamed_Figures.jpg/500px-Unnamed_Figures.jpg 1.5x" width="250" height="248" /></a>
<figcaption>Woodcut from "Articuli centum et sexaginta adversus huius tempestatis mathematicos atque philosophos", <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> 1588</figcaption>
</figure>

In Germany, he failed to obtain a teaching position at [Marburg](/wiki/Marburg "Marburg"), but was granted permission to teach at [Wittenberg](/wiki/University_of_Halle-Wittenberg "University of Halle-Wittenberg"){.mw-redirect}, where he lectured on [Aristotle](/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") for two years.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}42[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting1914196–197-42)^ However, with a change of intellectual climate there, he was no longer welcome, and went in 1588 to [Prague](/wiki/Prague "Prague"), where he obtained 300 [taler](/wiki/Thaler "Thaler") from [Rudolf II](/wiki/Rudolf_II "Rudolf II"){.mw-redirect}, but no teaching position.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}43[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting1914207–213-43)^ He went on to serve briefly as a professor in [Helmstedt](/wiki/University_of_Helmstedt "University of Helmstedt"), but had to flee again in 1590 when he was [excommunicated](/wiki/Excommunication "Excommunication") by the [Lutherans](/wiki/Lutheranism "Lutheranism").^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}44[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting1914214–219-44)^

During this period, he produced several [Latin](/wiki/Latin "Latin") works, dictated to his friend and secretary Girolamo Besler, including *De Magia* (*On Magic*), *Theses De Magia* (*Theses on Magic*) and *De Vinculis in Genere* (*A General Account of Bonding*). All these were apparently transcribed or recorded by Besler (or Bisler) between 1589 and 1590.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}45[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBruno1998xxxvi-45)^ He also published *De Imaginum, Signorum, Et Idearum Compositione* (*On the Composition of Images, Signs and Ideas*, 1591).

In 1591 he was in [Frankfurt](/wiki/Frankfurt "Frankfurt"), where he received an invitation from the [Venetian](/wiki/Venice "Venice") patrician [Giovanni Mocenigo](/wiki/Mocenigo_family "Mocenigo family"){.mw-redirect}, who wished to be instructed in the art of memory,^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}46[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting1914224–225-46)^ and also heard of a vacant chair in mathematics at the [University of Padua](/wiki/University_of_Padua "University of Padua"). At the time, the [Inquisition](/wiki/Inquisition "Inquisition") seemed to be losing some of its strictness, and because the [Republic of Venice](/wiki/Republic_of_Venice "Republic of Venice") was the most liberal state in the [Italian Peninsula](/wiki/Italian_Peninsula "Italian Peninsula"), Bruno was lulled into making the fatal mistake of returning to Italy.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}47[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-47)^

He went first to [Padua](/wiki/Padua "Padua"), where he taught briefly, and applied unsuccessfully for the chair of mathematics, which was given instead to [Galileo Galilei](/wiki/Galileo_Galilei "Galileo Galilei") one year later. Bruno accepted Mocenigo\'s invitation and moved to Venice in March 1592.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}48[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting1914249-48)^ For about two months, he served as an in-house tutor to Mocenigo, to whom he let slip some of his heterodox ideas.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}49[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting1914253–257-49)^ Mocenigo denounced him to the [Venetian Inquisition](/wiki/Venetian_Holy_Inquisition "Venetian Holy Inquisition"), which had Bruno arrested on 22 May 1592.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}50[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting1914257–258-50)^ Among the numerous charges of [blasphemy](/wiki/Blasphemy "Blasphemy") and [heresy](/wiki/Heresy "Heresy") brought against him in Venice, based on Mocenigo\'s denunciation, was his belief in the [plurality of worlds](/wiki/Cosmic_pluralism "Cosmic pluralism"), as well as accusations of personal misconduct.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}51[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting1914259-51)^ Bruno defended himself skillfully, stressing the philosophical character of some of his positions, denying others and admitting that he had had doubts on some matters of dogma. The Roman Inquisition, however, asked for his transfer to Rome.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}52[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting1914287–288-52)^ After several months of argument, the Venetian authorities reluctantly consented, and Bruno was sent to Rome in January 1593.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}53[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoulting1914292-53)^

### []{#Imprisonment.2C_trial_and_execution.2C_1593.E2.80.931600}Imprisonment, trial and execution, 1593--1600 {#Imprisonment,_trial_and_execution,_1593–1600}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Imprisonment, trial and execution, 1593–1600")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

During the seven years of his trial in Rome, Bruno was held in confinement, lastly in the [Tower of Nona](/wiki/Tower_of_Nona "Tower of Nona"){.mw-redirect}. Some important documents about the trial are lost, but others have been preserved, among them a summary of the proceedings that was rediscovered in 1940.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}54[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-54)^ The numerous charges against Bruno, based on some of his books as well as on witness accounts, included blasphemy, immoral conduct, and heresy in matters of dogmatic theology, and involved some of the basic doctrines of his philosophy and cosmology. [Luigi Firpo](/wiki/Luigi_Firpo "Luigi Firpo") speculates the charges made against Bruno by the Roman Inquisition were:^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}55[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirpo1993-55)^

-   holding opinions contrary to the [Catholic faith](/wiki/Catholicism "Catholicism"){.mw-redirect} and speaking against it and its ministers;
-   holding opinions contrary to the Catholic faith about the [Trinity](/wiki/Trinity "Trinity"), the [deity of Christ](/wiki/Deity_of_Christ "Deity of Christ"){.mw-redirect}, and the [Incarnation](/wiki/Incarnation_(Christianity) "Incarnation (Christianity)");
-   holding opinions contrary to the Catholic faith pertaining to Jesus as the [Christ](/wiki/Christ_(title) "Christ (title)");
-   holding opinions contrary to the Catholic faith regarding the [virginity of Mary, mother of Jesus](/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus) "Mary (mother of Jesus)"){.mw-redirect};
-   holding opinions contrary to the Catholic faith about both [Transubstantiation](/wiki/Transubstantiation "Transubstantiation") and the [Mass](/wiki/Mass_in_the_Catholic_Church "Mass in the Catholic Church");
-   claiming the existence of a [plurality of worlds](/wiki/Cosmic_pluralism "Cosmic pluralism") and [their eternity](/wiki/Eternity_of_the_world "Eternity of the world");
-   believing in [metempsychosis](/wiki/Metempsychosis "Metempsychosis") and in the [transmigration](/wiki/Transmigration_of_the_soul "Transmigration of the soul"){.mw-redirect} of the human soul into brutes;
-   dealing in magics and divination.

<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb">

<figcaption>The trial of Giordano Bruno by the Roman Inquisition; bronze relief by Ettore Ferrari, <a href="/wiki/Campo_de%27_Fiori" title="Campo de&#39; Fiori">Campo de' Fiori</a>, Rome</figcaption>
</figure>

Bruno defended himself as he had in Venice, insisting that he accepted the Church\'s dogmatic teachings, but trying to preserve the basis of his cosmological views. In particular, he held firm to his belief in the plurality of worlds, although he was admonished to abandon it. His trial was overseen by the Inquisitor Cardinal [Bellarmine](/wiki/Robert_Bellarmine "Robert Bellarmine"), who demanded a full recantation, which Bruno eventually refused. On 20 January 1600, [Pope Clement VIII](/wiki/Pope_Clement_VIII "Pope Clement VIII") declared Bruno a heretic, and the Inquisition issued a sentence of death. According to the correspondence of [Gaspar Schopp](/wiki/Caspar_Schoppe "Caspar Schoppe") of [Breslau](/wiki/Breslau "Breslau"){.mw-redirect}, he is said to have made a threatening gesture towards his judges and to have replied: *Maiori forsan cum timore sententiam in me fertis quam ego accipiam* (\"Perhaps you pronounce this sentence against me with greater fear than I receive it\").^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}56[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-56)^

He was turned over to the secular authorities. On 17 February 1600, in the [Campo de\' Fiori](/wiki/Campo_de%27_Fiori "Campo de' Fiori") (a central Roman market square), naked, with his \"tongue imprisoned because of his wicked words\", he was [burned alive at the stake](/wiki/Death_by_burning "Death by burning").^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}57[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-57)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}58[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-58)^ His ashes were thrown into the [Tiber](/wiki/Tiber "Tiber") river.

All of Bruno\'s works were placed on the *[Index Librorum Prohibitorum](/wiki/Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum "Index Librorum Prohibitorum")* in 1603. The inquisition cardinals who judged Giordano Bruno were [Cardinal Bellarmino (Bellarmine)](/wiki/Robert_Bellarmine "Robert Bellarmine"), [Cardinal Madruzzo (Madruzzi)](/wiki/Carlo_Gaudenzio_Madruzzo "Carlo Gaudenzio Madruzzo"), Cardinal Camillo Borghese (later [Pope Paul V](/wiki/Pope_Paul_V "Pope Paul V")), Domenico Cardinal Pinelli, Pompeio Cardinal Arrigoni, [Cardinal Sfondrati](/wiki/Paolo_Emilio_Sfondrati "Paolo Emilio Sfondrati"), [Pedro Cardinal De Deza Manuel](/wiki/Pedro_de_Deza "Pedro de Deza") and [Cardinal Santorio](/wiki/Giulio_Antonio_Santorio "Giulio Antonio Santorio") (Archbishop of Santa Severina, Cardinal-Bishop of Palestrina).^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}59[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-59)^

The measures taken to prevent Bruno from continuing to speak have resulted in his becoming a symbol for free thought and [free speech](/wiki/Free_speech "Free speech"){.mw-redirect} in present-day Rome, where an annual memorial service takes place close to the spot where he was executed.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}60[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTERowland2016%5BhttpsbooksgooglecombooksidC-q8CwAAQBAJpgPT8_8%5D-60)^

### Physical appearance {#Physical_appearance}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Physical appearance")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

The earliest likeness of Bruno is an engraving published in 1715^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}61[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-61)^ and cited by Salvestrini as \"the only known portrait of Bruno\". Salvestrini suggests that it is a re-engraving made from a now lost original.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}23[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-Salvestrini-1958-23)^ This engraving has provided the source for later images.

The records of Bruno\'s imprisonment by the Venetian Inquisition in May 1592 describe him as a man \"of average height, with a hazel-coloured beard and the appearance of being about forty years of age\". Alternately, a passage in a work by [George Abbot](/wiki/George_Abbot_(Archbishop_of_Canterbury) "George Abbot (Archbishop of Canterbury)"){.mw-redirect} indicates that Bruno was of diminutive stature: \"When that Italian Didapper, who intituled himself Philotheus Iordanus Brunus Nolanus, magis elaboratae Theologiae Doctor, &c. with a name longer than his body\...\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}62[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-62)^

## Cosmology {#Cosmology}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Cosmology")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

### Contemporary cosmological beliefs {#Contemporary_cosmological_beliefs}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Contemporary cosmological beliefs")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

See also: [Celestial spheres § History](/wiki/Celestial_spheres#History "Celestial spheres")

<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb">
<a href="/wiki/File:Bartolomeu_Velho_1568.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bartolomeu_Velho_1568.jpg/500px-Bartolomeu_Velho_1568.jpg" class="mw-file-element" data-file-height="2402" data-file-width="3255" decoding="async" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bartolomeu_Velho_1568.jpg/960px-Bartolomeu_Velho_1568.jpg 1.5x" width="380" height="280" /></a>
<figcaption>Illuminated illustration of the Ptolemaic geocentric conception of the universe. The outermost text reads "The heavenly empire, dwelling of God and all the elect."</figcaption>
</figure>

In the first half of the 15th century, [Nicholas of Cusa](/wiki/Nicholas_of_Cusa "Nicholas of Cusa") challenged the then widely accepted philosophies of [Aristotelianism](/wiki/Aristotelianism "Aristotelianism"), envisioning instead an infinite universe whose centre was everywhere and circumference nowhere, and moreover teeming with countless stars.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}63[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-63)^ He also predicted that neither were the rotational orbits circular nor were their movements uniform.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}64[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-64)^

In the second half of the 16th century, the theories of Copernicus (1473--1543) began to diffuse through Europe. Copernicus conserved the idea of planets fixed to solid spheres, but considered the apparent motion of the stars to be an illusion caused by the rotation of the Earth on its axis; he also preserved the notion of an immobile centre, but it was the Sun rather than the Earth. Copernicus also argued that the Earth was a planet orbiting the Sun once every year. However, he maintained the [Ptolemaic hypothesis](/wiki/Ptolemaic_model "Ptolemaic model"){.mw-redirect} that the orbits of the planets were composed of perfect circles---[deferents](/wiki/Deferents "Deferents"){.mw-redirect} and [epicycles](/wiki/Epicycles "Epicycles"){.mw-redirect}---and that the stars were fixed on a stationary outer sphere.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}65[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-65)^

Despite the widespread publication of Copernicus\' work *[De revolutionibus orbium coelestium](/wiki/De_revolutionibus_orbium_coelestium "De revolutionibus orbium coelestium")*, during Bruno\'s time most educated Catholics subscribed to the Aristotelian [geocentric](/wiki/Geocentrism "Geocentrism") view that the Earth was the [centre of the universe](/wiki/History_of_the_Center_of_the_Universe "History of the Center of the Universe"){.mw-redirect}, and that all heavenly bodies revolved around it.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}66[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-66)^ The ultimate limit of the universe was the *primum mobile*, whose diurnal rotation was conferred upon it by a [transcendental](/wiki/Transcendence_(religion) "Transcendence (religion)") God, not part of the universe (although, as the [kingdom of heaven](/wiki/Empyrean "Empyrean"), [adjacent to it](/wiki/Celestial_spheres "Celestial spheres")^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}67[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-67)^), a motionless [prime mover](/wiki/Cosmological_argument "Cosmological argument") and [first cause](/wiki/First_cause "First cause"){.mw-redirect}. The fixed stars were part of this celestial sphere, all at the same fixed distance from the immobile Earth at the centre of the sphere. [Ptolemy](/wiki/Ptolemy "Ptolemy") had numbered these at 1,022, grouped into 48 [constellations](/wiki/Constellation "Constellation"). The [planets](/wiki/Planet "Planet") were each fixed to a transparent sphere.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}68[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-68)^

Few [astronomers](/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer") of Bruno\'s time accepted [Copernicus\'s heliocentric model](/wiki/Copernican_heliocentrism "Copernican heliocentrism"). Among those who did were the Germans [Michael Maestlin](/wiki/Michael_Maestlin "Michael Maestlin") (1550--1631), [Christoph Rothmann](/wiki/Christoph_Rothmann "Christoph Rothmann"), [Johannes Kepler](/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") (1571--1630); the Englishman [Thomas Digges](/wiki/Thomas_Digges "Thomas Digges") (c. 1546--1595), author of *A Perfit Description of the Caelestial Orbes*; and the Italian [Galileo Galilei](/wiki/Galileo_Galilei "Galileo Galilei") (1564--1642).

### Cosmological claims {#Cosmological_claims}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Cosmological claims")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

Main article: [Heliocentrism](/wiki/Heliocentrism "Heliocentrism")

In 1584, Bruno published two important philosophical dialogues (*La Cena de le Ceneri* and *De l\'infinito universo et mondi*) in which he argued against the planetary spheres ([Christoph Rothmann](/wiki/Christoph_Rothmann "Christoph Rothmann") did the same in 1586 as did [Tycho Brahe](/wiki/Tycho_Brahe "Tycho Brahe") in 1587) and affirmed the Copernican principle.

In particular, to support the Copernican view and oppose the objection according to which the motion of the Earth would be perceived by means of the motion of winds, clouds etc., in *La Cena de le Ceneri* Bruno anticipates some of the arguments of Galileo on the relativity principle.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}69[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-69)^ Note that he also uses the example now known as [Galileo\'s ship](/wiki/Galileo%27s_ship "Galileo's ship").

> Theophilus -- \[\...\] air through which the clouds and winds move are parts of the Earth, \[\...\] to mean under the name of Earth the whole machinery and the entire animated part, which consists of dissimilar parts; so that the rivers, the rocks, the seas, the whole vaporous and turbulent air, which is enclosed within the highest mountains, should belong to the Earth as its members, just as the air \[does\] in the lungs and in other cavities of animals by which they breathe, widen their arteries, and other similar effects necessary for life are performed. The clouds, too, move through accidents in the body of the Earth and are in its bowels as are the waters. \[\...\] With the Earth move \[\...\] all things that are on the Earth. If, therefore, from a point outside the Earth something were thrown upon the Earth, it would lose, because of the latter\'s motion, its straightness as would be seen on the ship \[\...\] moving along a river, if someone on point C of the riverbank were to throw a stone along a straight line, and would see the stone miss its target by the amount of the velocity of the ship\'s motion. But if someone were placed high on the mast of that ship, move as it may however fast, he would not miss his target at all, so that the stone or some other heavy thing thrown downward would not come along a straight line from the point E which is at the top of the mast, or cage, to the point D which is at the bottom of the mast, or at some point in the bowels and body of the ship. Thus, if from the point D to the point E someone who is inside the ship would throw a stone straight up, it would return to the bottom along the same line, however far the ship moved, provided it was not subject to any pitch and roll.\"^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}70[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-70)^

Bruno\'s infinite universe was filled with a substance---a \"pure air\", [aether](/wiki/Aether_(classical_element) "Aether (classical element)"), or *spiritus*---that offered no resistance to the heavenly bodies which, in Bruno\'s view, rather than being fixed, moved under their own [impetus](/wiki/Impetus_(mechanics) "Impetus (mechanics)"){.mw-redirect} (momentum). Most dramatically, he completely abandoned the idea of a [hierarchical](/wiki/Hierarchy "Hierarchy") universe.

> The universe is then one, infinite, immobile\... It is not capable of comprehension and therefore is endless and limitless, and to that extent infinite and indeterminable, and consequently immobile.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}71[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-71)^

Bruno\'s cosmology distinguishes between \"suns\" which produce their own light and heat, and have other bodies moving around them; and \"earths\" which move around suns and receive light and heat from them.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}72[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-Bruno-1584-72)^ Bruno suggested that some, if not all, of the objects classically known as [fixed stars](/wiki/Fixed_stars "Fixed stars") are in fact suns.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}72[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-Bruno-1584-72)^ According to astrophysicist [Steven Soter](/wiki/Steven_Soter "Steven Soter"), he was the first person to grasp that \"stars are other suns with their own planets\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}73[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-73)^

Bruno wrote that other worlds \"have no less virtue nor a nature different from that of our Earth\" and, like Earth, \"contain animals and inhabitants\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}74[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-74)^

During the late 16th century and throughout the 17th century, Bruno\'s ideas were held up for ridicule, debate, or inspiration. [Margaret Cavendish](/wiki/Margaret_Cavendish "Margaret Cavendish"){.mw-redirect}, for example, wrote an entire series of poems against \"atoms\" and \"infinite worlds\" in *Poems and Fancies* in 1664. Bruno\'s true, if partial, vindication would have to wait for the implications and impact of [Newtonian](/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") cosmology.

Bruno\'s overall contribution to the birth of modern science is still controversial. Some scholars follow Frances Yates in stressing the importance of Bruno\'s ideas about the universe being infinite and lacking a geocentric structure as a crucial crossing point between the old and the new. Others see in Bruno\'s idea of multiple worlds instantiating the infinite possibilities of a pristine, indivisible One,^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}75[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-75)^ a forerunner of [Everett](/wiki/Hugh_Everett "Hugh Everett"){.mw-redirect}\'s [many-worlds interpretation](/wiki/Many-worlds_interpretation "Many-worlds interpretation") of quantum mechanics.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}76[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-76)^

While many academics note Bruno\'s theological position as [pantheism](/wiki/Pantheism "Pantheism"), several have described it as [pandeism](/wiki/Pandeism "Pandeism"), and some also as [panentheism](/wiki/Panentheism "Panentheism").^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}77[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-77)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}78[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-78)^ Physicist and philosopher [Max Bernhard Weinstein](/wiki/Max_Bernhard_Weinstein "Max Bernhard Weinstein") in his *Welt- und Lebensanschauungen, Hervorgegangen aus Religion, Philosophie und Naturerkenntnis* (\"[World and Life Views, Emerging From Religion, Philosophy and Nature](https://archive.org/details/World-and-Life-Views){.external .text rel="nofollow"}\"), wrote that the theological model of [pandeism](/wiki/Pandeism "Pandeism") was strongly expressed in the teachings of Bruno, especially with respect to the vision of a deity for which \"the concept of God is not separated from that of the universe\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}79[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-79)^ However, [Otto Kern](/wiki/Otto_Kern "Otto Kern") takes exception to what he considers Weinstein\'s overbroad assertions that Bruno, as well as other historical philosophers such as [John Scotus Eriugena](/wiki/John_Scotus_Eriugena "John Scotus Eriugena"), [Nicholas of Cusa](/wiki/Nicholas_of_Cusa "Nicholas of Cusa"), [Mendelssohn](/wiki/Moses_Mendelssohn "Moses Mendelssohn"), and [Lessing](/wiki/Gotthold_Ephraim_Lessing "Gotthold Ephraim Lessing"), were pandeists or leaned towards pandeism.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}80[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-80)^ *[Discover](/wiki/Discover_(magazine) "Discover (magazine)")* editor [Corey S. Powell](/wiki/Corey_S._Powell "Corey S. Powell") also described Bruno\'s [cosmology](/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology") as pandeistic, writing that it was \"a tool for advancing an animist or Pandeist theology\",^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}81[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-81)^ and this assessment of Bruno as a pandeist was agreed with by science writer Michael Newton Keas,^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}82[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-82)^ and *[The Daily Beast](/wiki/The_Daily_Beast "The Daily Beast")* writer David Sessions.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}83[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-83)^

### Late Vatican position {#Late_Vatican_position}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Late Vatican position")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

The Vatican has published a few official statements about Bruno\'s trial and execution. In 1942, Cardinal [Giovanni Mercati](/wiki/Giovanni_Mercati "Giovanni Mercati"), who discovered a number of lost documents relating to Bruno\'s trial, stated that the Church was perfectly justified in condemning him.^\[*[[citation\ needed]{title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2022)"}](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ On the 400th anniversary of Bruno\'s death, in 2000, Cardinal [Angelo Sodano](/wiki/Angelo_Sodano "Angelo Sodano") declared Bruno\'s death to be a \"sad episode\" but, despite his regret, he defended Bruno\'s prosecutors, maintaining that the Inquisitors \"had the desire to serve freedom and promote the common good and did everything possible to save his life\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}84[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-84)^ In the same year, Pope [John Paul II](/wiki/John_Paul_II "John Paul II"){.mw-redirect} made a general apology for \"the use of violence that some have committed in the service of truth\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}85[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-85)^

### A martyr of science {#A_martyr_of_science}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: A martyr of science")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

See also: [Conflict thesis](/wiki/Conflict_thesis "Conflict thesis")

Some authors have characterised Bruno as a \"martyr of science\", suggesting parallels with the [Galileo affair](/wiki/Galileo_affair "Galileo affair") which began around 1610.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}86[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-86)^ \"It should not be supposed,\" writes A. M. Paterson of Bruno and his \"heliocentric solar system\", that he \"reached his conclusions via some mystical revelation \... His work is an essential part of the scientific and philosophical developments that he initiated.\"^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}87[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-87)^ Paterson echoes [Hegel](/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel "Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel") in writing that Bruno \"ushers in a modern theory of knowledge that understands all natural things in the universe to be known by the human mind through the mind\'s dialectical structure\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}88[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-88)^

Ingegno writes that Bruno embraced the philosophy of [Lucretius](/wiki/Lucretius "Lucretius"), \"aimed at liberating man from the fear of death and the gods\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}89[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBruno1998%22Introduction%22-89)^ Characters in Bruno\'s *Cause, Principle and Unity* desire \"to improve speculative science and knowledge of natural things,\" and to achieve a philosophy \"which brings about the perfection of the human intellect most easily and eminently, and most closely corresponds to the truth of nature\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}90[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBruno199863-90)^

Other scholars oppose such views and claim Bruno\'s martyrdom to science to be exaggerated or outright false. For Yates, while \"nineteenth-century liberals\" were thrown \"into ecstasies\" over Bruno\'s Copernicanism, \"Bruno pushes Copernicus\' scientific work back into a prescientific stage, back into Hermeticism, interpreting the Copernican diagram as a hieroglyph of divine mysteries.\"^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}91[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYates1964225-91)^

According to historian Mordechai Feingold, \"Both admirers and critics of Giordano Bruno basically agree that he was pompous and arrogant, highly valuing his opinions and showing little patience with anyone who even mildly disagreed with him.\" Discussing Bruno\'s experience of rejection when he visited Oxford University, Feingold suggests that \"it might have been Bruno\'s manner, his language and his self-assertiveness, rather than his ideas\" that caused offence.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}92[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-92)^

### Theological heresy {#Theological_heresy}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Theological heresy")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

In his *[Lectures on the History of Philosophy](/wiki/Lectures_on_the_History_of_Philosophy "Lectures on the History of Philosophy")*, [Hegel](/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel "Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel") writes that Bruno\'s life represented \"a bold rejection of all [Catholic](/wiki/Catholic "Catholic"){.mw-redirect} beliefs resting on mere authority\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}93[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-93)^

Alfonso Ingegno states that Bruno\'s philosophy \"challenges the developments of the Reformation, calls into question the truth-value of the whole of Christianity, and claims that Christ perpetrated a deceit on mankind \... Bruno suggests that we can now recognize the universal law which controls the perpetual becoming of all things in an infinite universe.\"^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}94[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBruno1998x-94)^ A. M. Paterson says that, while we no longer have a copy of the official papal condemnation of Bruno, his heresies included \"the doctrine of the infinite universe and the innumerable worlds\" and his beliefs \"on the movement of the earth\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}95[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-95)^

Michael White notes that the Inquisition may have pursued Bruno early in his life on the basis of his opposition to [Aristotle](/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle"), interest in [Arianism](/wiki/Arianism "Arianism"), reading of [Erasmus](/wiki/Desiderius_Erasmus "Desiderius Erasmus"){.mw-redirect}, and possession of banned texts.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}96[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhite20027-96)^ White considers that Bruno\'s later heresy was \"multifaceted\" and may have rested on his conception of infinite worlds. \"This was perhaps the most dangerous notion of all \... If other worlds existed with intelligent beings living there, did they, too, have their visitations? The idea was quite unthinkable.\"^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}96[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhite20027-96)^

[Frances Yates](/wiki/Frances_Yates "Frances Yates") rejects what she describes as the \"legend that Bruno was prosecuted as a philosophical thinker, was burned for his daring views on innumerable worlds or on the movement of the earth.\" Yates, however, writes that \"the Church was \... perfectly within its rights if it included philosophical points in its condemnation of Bruno\'s heresies\" because \"the philosophical points were quite inseparable from the heresies\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}97[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYates1964354–356-97)^

According to the *[Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy](/wiki/Stanford_Encyclopedia_of_Philosophy "Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy")*, \"in 1600 there was no official Catholic position on the Copernican system, and it was certainly not a heresy. When \[\...\] Bruno \[\...\] was burned at the stake as a heretic, it had nothing to do with his writings in support of Copernican cosmology.\"^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}98[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-98)^

The website of the [Vatican Apostolic Archive](/wiki/Vatican_Apostolic_Archive "Vatican Apostolic Archive"), discussing a summary of legal proceedings against Bruno in Rome, states:

> In the same rooms where Giordano Bruno was questioned, for the same important reasons of the relationship between science and faith, at the dawning of the new astronomy and at the decline of Aristotle\'s philosophy, sixteen years later, [Cardinal Bellarmino](/wiki/Robert_Bellarmine "Robert Bellarmine"), who then contested Bruno\'s heretical theses, summoned Galileo Galilei, who also faced a famous inquisitorial trial, which, luckily for him, ended with a simple abjuration.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}99[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-99)^

Galileo ultimately recanted his views and agreed to [house arrest](/wiki/House_arrest "House arrest") and was spared from being burned at the stake, while Bruno held his positions until death.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}100[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-100)^ The concepts of [exoplanets](/wiki/Exoplanets "Exoplanets"){.mw-redirect}, the idea that the [shape of the universe](/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe") goes on [infinitely](/wiki/Infinitely "Infinitely"){.mw-redirect}, and the [Solar System](/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") holding no [cosmic importance](/wiki/Meaning_(philosophy) "Meaning (philosophy)") or center, would later become major concepts in the field of [cosmology](/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"), as well as philosophy.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}101[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-101)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}102[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-102)^

## Cultural legacy {#Cultural_legacy}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Cultural legacy")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

### In art {#In_art}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: In art")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

Following the 1870 [Capture of Rome](/wiki/Capture_of_Rome "Capture of Rome") by the newly created [Kingdom of Italy](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy "Kingdom of Italy") and the end of the Church\'s [temporal power](/wiki/Temporal_power_(Papal) "Temporal power (Papal)"){.mw-redirect} over the city, the erection of a [monument to Bruno](/wiki/Monument_to_Giordano_Bruno "Monument to Giordano Bruno") on the site of his execution became feasible. The monument was sharply opposed by the clerical party, but was finally erected by the Rome Municipality and inaugurated in 1889.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}103[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-103)^ The statue depicts a robed and hooded monk in mid-stride, a book held in his right hand against his left thigh. The inscription below reads:

**IX GIVGNO MDCCCLXXXIX**

**---------**

**A BRVNO**

**IL SECOLO DA LVI DIVINATO**

**QVI**

**DOVE IL ROGO ARSE**

**9 June 1889**

**---------**

**To Bruno**

**From the age he divined**

**Here**

**Where the pyre burned**

A statue of a stretched human figure standing on its head, designed by [Alexander Polzin](/wiki/Alexander_Polzin "Alexander Polzin") and depicting Bruno\'s death at the stake, was placed in [Potsdamer Platz](/wiki/Potsdamer_Platz "Potsdamer Platz") station in Berlin on 2 March 2008.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}105[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-105)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}106[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-106)^

Retrospective iconography of Bruno shows him with a Dominican [cowl](/wiki/Cowl "Cowl") but not [tonsured](/wiki/Tonsure "Tonsure"). Edward Gosselin has suggested that it is likely Bruno kept his tonsure at least until 1579, and it is possible that he wore it again thereafter.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}107[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-107)^

An idealised animated version of Bruno appears in the first episode of the 2014 television series *[Cosmos: A Spacetime Odyssey](/wiki/Cosmos:_A_Spacetime_Odyssey "Cosmos: A Spacetime Odyssey")*. In this depiction, Bruno is shown with a more modern look, without tonsure and wearing clerical robes and without his hood. *Cosmos* presents Bruno as an impoverished philosopher who was ultimately executed due to his refusal to recant his belief in other worlds, a portrayal that was criticised by some as simplistic or historically inaccurate.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}108[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-Powell-2014-108)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}109[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-109)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}110[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-110)^ Corey S. Powell, of *Discover* magazine, says of Bruno, \"A major reason he moved around so much is that he was argumentative, sarcastic, and drawn to controversy \... He was a brilliant, complicated, difficult man.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}108[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-Powell-2014-108)^

### In poetry {#In_poetry}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: In poetry")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

Poems that refer to Bruno include:

-   \"The Monument of Giordano Bruno\" (1889) by [Algernon Charles Swinburne](/wiki/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne "Algernon Charles Swinburne"), written when the statue of Bruno was constructed in Rome.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}111[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-111)^
-   \"Campo Dei Fiori\" (1943) by [Czesław Miłosz](/wiki/Czes%C5%82aw_Mi%C5%82osz "Czesław Miłosz"), which draws parallels between indifference to the fate of Bruno and indifference to the victims of the then-ongoing [Occupation of Poland](/wiki/Occupation_of_Poland_(1939%E2%80%931945) "Occupation of Poland (1939–1945)").^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}112[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-112)^
-   \"The Emancipators\" (1958) by [Randall Jarrell](/wiki/Randall_Jarrell "Randall Jarrell"), which addresses Bruno, along with Galileo and Newton, as an originator of the modern scientific-industrial world.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}113[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-113)^
-   \"What He Thought\" (1994) by [Heather McHugh](/wiki/Heather_McHugh "Heather McHugh"), a (possibly autobiographical) poem about a group of American poets who visit Italy and are lectured about Bruno and the nature of poetry by a local arts administrator. The poem was published in the collection *Hinge & Sign*, a nominee for the [National Book Award](/wiki/National_Book_Award "National Book Award").^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}114[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-114)^

### In fiction {#In_fiction}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: In fiction")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

Bruno and his theory of \"the coincidence of contraries\" (*coincidentia oppositorum*) play an important role in [James Joyce](/wiki/James_Joyce "James Joyce")\'s 1939 novel *[Finnegans Wake](/wiki/Finnegans_Wake "Finnegans Wake")*. Joyce wrote in a letter to his patroness, [Harriet Shaw Weaver](/wiki/Harriet_Shaw_Weaver "Harriet Shaw Weaver"), \"His philosophy is a kind of dualism -- every power in nature must evolve an opposite in order to realise itself and opposition brings reunion\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}115[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-115)^ Amongst his numerous allusions to Bruno in his novel, including his trial and torture, Joyce plays upon Bruno\'s notion of *coincidentia oppositorum* through applying his name to word puns such as \"Browne and Nolan\" (the name of Dublin printers) and \'\"brownesberrow in nolandsland\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}116[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-116)^

In 1973 the biographical drama *[Giordano Bruno](/wiki/Giordano_Bruno_(film) "Giordano Bruno (film)")* was released, an Italian/French movie directed by [Giuliano Montaldo](/wiki/Giuliano_Montaldo "Giuliano Montaldo"), starring [Gian Maria Volonté](/wiki/Gian_Maria_Volont%C3%A9 "Gian Maria Volonté") as Bruno.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}117[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-117)^

Bruno is a major character in the four-novel [Aegypt](/wiki/Aegypt "Aegypt"){.mw-redirect} sequence (1987--2007) by [John Crowley](/wiki/John_Crowley_(author) "John Crowley (author)"). Historical episodes from Bruno\'s life are fictionalised in the novels, and his philosophical ideas are key to the novels' themes.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}118[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-Publishers_Weekly_1987-118)^

*The Last Confession* (2000) by [Morris West](/wiki/Morris_West "Morris West") is an unfinished, posthumously published fictional autobiography of Bruno, ostensibly written shortly before Bruno\'s execution.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}119[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-119)^

In the 2008 novel [*Children of God*](/wiki/Children_of_God_(novel) "Children of God (novel)") by [Mary Doria Russell](/wiki/Mary_Doria_Russell "Mary Doria Russell"), several characters travel on an interstellar spaceship named *Giordano Bruno*.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}120[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-Powell's_Books_2022-120)^

Bruno features as the hero of the *Giordano Bruno* series (2010--2023) of historical crime novels by S. J. Parris (a pseudonym of [Stephanie Merritt](/wiki/Stephanie_Merritt "Stephanie Merritt")).^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}121[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-121)^

### In music {#In_music}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: In music")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

[Hans Werner Henze](/wiki/Hans_Werner_Henze "Hans Werner Henze") set his large scale cantata for orchestra, choir and four soloists, *Novae de infinito laudes* to Italian texts by Bruno, recorded in 1972 at the Salzburg Festival reissued on CD Orfeo C609 031B.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}122[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-122)^

The Italian composer [Francesco Filidei](/wiki/Francesco_Filidei "Francesco Filidei") wrote an opera, based on a libretto by Stefano Busellato, titled *Giordano Bruno*. The premiere took place on 12 September 2015 at the [Casa da Música](/wiki/Casa_da_M%C3%BAsica "Casa da Música") in Porto, Portugal.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}123[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-123)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}124[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-124)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}125[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-125)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}126[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-126)^

The 2016 song \"Roman Sky\" by heavy metal band [Avenged Sevenfold](/wiki/Avenged_Sevenfold "Avenged Sevenfold") focuses on the death of Bruno.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}127[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-127)^

Bruno is the central character in [Roger Doyle](/wiki/Roger_Doyle "Roger Doyle")\'s *Heresy -- an electronic opera* (2017).^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}128[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-128)^

### In television {#In_television}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: In television")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

[Chumlee](/wiki/Chumlee "Chumlee") tracking down a first edition copy of a book by Bruno in Rome is a part of a [season 17 episode](/wiki/List_of_Pawn_Stars_episodes "List of Pawn Stars episodes") of the American TV series *[Pawn Stars](/wiki/Pawn_Stars "Pawn Stars")*.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}129[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-129)^

The *[Doctor Who](/wiki/Doctor_Who "Doctor Who")* serial *[The Ribos Operation](/wiki/The_Ribos_Operation "The Ribos Operation")* (1978) features a character named \"Binro the Heretic\", who was ostracised by his people for claiming the stars were not ice crystals, but other suns. The [BBC](/wiki/BBC "BBC") has explicitly drawn a connection between the two.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}130[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-130)^

In the first episode of the show [Cosmos: A Spacetime Odyssey](/wiki/Cosmos:_A_Spacetime_Odyssey "Cosmos: A Spacetime Odyssey"), the story of Giordano Bruno and his vision of an infinite universe is narrated by the host [Neil deGrasse Tyson](/wiki/Neil_deGrasse_Tyson "Neil deGrasse Tyson") using computer-generated animations.

## Legacy {#Legacy}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Legacy")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

### Giordano Bruno Foundation {#Giordano_Bruno_Foundation}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Giordano Bruno Foundation")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

Main article: [Giordano Bruno Foundation](/wiki/Giordano_Bruno_Foundation "Giordano Bruno Foundation")

The Giordano Bruno Foundation (German: Giordano-Bruno-Stiftung) is a non-profit foundation based in Germany that pursues the \"Support of [Evolutionary Humanism](/wiki/Evolutionary_Humanism "Evolutionary Humanism"){.mw-redirect}\". It was founded by entrepreneur Herbert Steffen in 2004. The Giordano Bruno Foundation is critical of [religious fundamentalism](/wiki/Religious_fundamentalism "Religious fundamentalism"){.mw-redirect} and nationalism.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}131[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-131)^

### Giordano Bruno Memorial Award {#Giordano_Bruno_Memorial_Award}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Giordano Bruno Memorial Award")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

The [SETI League](/wiki/Search_for_extraterrestrial_intelligence "Search for extraterrestrial intelligence") makes an annual award honouring the memory of Giordano Bruno to a deserving person or persons who have made a significant contribution to the practice of [SETI](/wiki/Search_for_extraterrestrial_intelligence "Search for extraterrestrial intelligence") (the search for extraterrestrial intelligence). The award was proposed by sociologist Donald Tarter in 1995 on the 395th anniversary of Bruno\'s death. The trophy presented is called a Bruno.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}132[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-132)^

### Astronomical objects named after Bruno {#Astronomical_objects_named_after_Bruno}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Giordano_Bruno&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Astronomical objects named after Bruno")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}

The 22 km impact crater [Giordano Bruno](/wiki/Giordano_Bruno_(crater) "Giordano Bruno (crater)") on the far side of the Moon is named in his honor, as are the main belt [Asteroids](/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid") [5148 Giordano](/wiki/5148_Giordano "5148 Giordano") and [13223 Cenaceneri](/wiki/13223_Cenaceneri "13223 Cenaceneri"){.mw-redirect}; the latter is named after his philosophical dialogue *La Cena de le Ceneri* (\"The Ash Wednesday Supper\") (see above).^\[*[[citation\ needed]{title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2024)"}](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^

In remembering this borrowing, we were struck by the vast expanse given the term. According to page 284, Scotus Erigena is one entirely, at p. 300 Anselm of Canterbury is \'half Pandeist\'; but also Nicholas of Cusa and Giordano Bruno, and even in Mendelssohn and Lessing a kind of Pandeism is found (p. 306 321 346.)\".]{.reference-text}]{#cite_note-80}
