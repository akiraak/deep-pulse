# Albert Camus on the Absurd: The Myth of Sisyphus

## Summary

This essay explores Albert Camus's philosophy of the absurd as presented in *The Myth of Sisyphus* (1942). The core argument centers on a fundamental human conflict: we desire meaning, justice, and understanding, yet the universe offers none of these things.

## Key Concepts

**The Absurd's Origin**: Camus defines the absurd as arising from the clash between human desires for meaning and the world's indifference to those desires. As the essay states, it involves "a mind that desires and the world that disappoints."

**Metaphysical and Epistemological Dimensions**: The absurd has two aspects—the universe's fundamental meaninglessness and our inability to comprehend why things happen as they do.

**Rejecting False Solutions**: Camus critiques existentialists like Kierkegaard who propose "leaps of faith" to escape absurdity. He argues such approaches contradict themselves by claiming life is absurd while offering transcendent solutions.

**Sisyphus as the Absurd Hero**: The mythological figure condemned to roll a boulder eternally represents humanity's condition. Camus argues Sisyphus achieves happiness through conscious revolt—embracing his fate with passion and freedom rather than seeking escape.

## Camus's Answer

Rather than suicide, Camus advocates living with full awareness of life's meaninglessness. Through passion, freedom, and revolt against despair, individuals can create their own fulfillment without relying on external meaning or hope.
