# Albert Camus on Rebelling against Life's Absurdity

Albert Camus examines whether existence is worth living, presenting life's absurdity through the myth of Sisyphus. He argues that we occupy an impossible space between our hunger for meaning and the universe's silent refusal to provide it.

## The Absurd Condition

Camus identifies a fundamental human inconsistency: we oscillate between feeling that life matters deeply and suddenly sensing that nothing signifies anything. He writes that "there is but one truly serious philosophical problem, and that is suicide."

The philosopher uses Sisyphus—condemned to eternally push a boulder uphill only to watch it roll down—as a metaphor for human existence. Our repetitive daily routines mirror this punishment. We construct meaning-giving frameworks, they inevitably collapse, and we compulsively begin again.

## The Problem of Knowledge

Camus emphasizes that ultimate cosmic meaning, if it exists, remains fundamentally unknowable to us. We cannot reconcile our appetite for absolute truth with reality's refusal to provide rational answers. This confrontation between human need and "the unreasonable silence of the world" creates the absurd.

## Metaphysical Rebellion

Rather than escape through delusion or despair, Camus advocates "metaphysical rebellion"—living consciously despite absurdity. He suggests we can reclaim our narrative by refusing comforting dogmas that offer false authenticity.

## Sisyphus as Hero

Camus reframes Sisyphus not as victim but as hero. Trudging down the mountainside after each failure, Sisyphus achieves full tragic consciousness while maintaining silent joy. His rock becomes his own; he chooses to continue.

By approaching life with intensity and lucidity, defying nihilism through conscious striving, we can establish authentic happiness. Camus concludes: "One must imagine Sisyphus happy."
